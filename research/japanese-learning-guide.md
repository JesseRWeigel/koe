# Learning Japanese as an English Speaker: A Comprehensive Guide

Japanese is classified by the US Foreign Service Institute as a **Category IV language** — the hardest category for English speakers, requiring roughly **2,200 class hours** to reach professional proficiency. This guide distills current community consensus on what actually works.

---

## 1. Writing Systems

Japanese uses three writing systems simultaneously. You must learn all three. There are no shortcuts here.

### 1.1 Kana: Hiragana and Katakana

**Learn these first. No excuses. No romaji crutches.**

- **Hiragana** (ひらがな): 46 base characters. Used for native Japanese words, grammar particles, verb endings. Learn this in your first week.
- **Katakana** (カタカナ): 46 base characters. Used for loanwords, foreign names, emphasis, onomatopoeia. Learn this in your second week.

**Best methods:**
- **Tofugu's mnemonics guide** (free) — widely considered the fastest approach, 2-4 days per script
- **Write them by hand** — even if you'll mostly type, the muscle memory helps cement recognition
- **Anki kana decks** — useful for reinforcement but not as primary learning

**Do NOT:**
- Rely on romaji (Latin alphabet transcription). It actively harms your learning by preventing you from thinking in Japanese sounds.
- Spend more than 2 weeks on kana. They should be automatic before you move on, but perfection comes through use, not drilling.

### 1.2 Kanji

Kanji is the biggest mountain in Japanese. There is no way around it.

**How many do you need?**

| Level | Kanji Count | Cumulative | What It Gets You |
|-------|------------|------------|------------------|
| JLPT N5 | 80 | 80 | Absolute basics — numbers, days, simple verbs |
| JLPT N4 | 170 | 250 | Elementary — can read simple signs, menus |
| JLPT N3 | 370 | 620 | Intermediate — can follow manga with a dictionary |
| JLPT N2 | 380 | 1,000 | Upper intermediate — can read newspapers with effort |
| JLPT N1 | 1,136 | 2,136 | Advanced — full Joyo kanji set |

The **Joyo kanji** (常用漢字) list contains **2,136 characters** designated by the Japanese Ministry of Education as necessary for literacy. This is the target for full reading ability. In practice, ~1,000 kanji (roughly N2 level) lets you read most everyday content with occasional lookups.

### 1.3 Kanji Learning Methods Compared

#### RTK (Remembering the Kanji) by James Heisig

**What it does:** Teaches you to recognize and write all 2,200+ kanji by associating each with an English keyword and a mnemonic story. Does NOT teach readings or vocabulary.

**Pros:**
- Fastest path to "I can distinguish all common kanji" — completable in 2-4 months
- Builds strong visual discrimination ability
- Mnemonic stories make characters memorable
- The immersion community's traditional go-to method

**Cons:**
- You learn zero Japanese readings — you still can't read anything after finishing
- English keywords create a translation bottleneck
- Requires pairing with a separate vocabulary study method
- Some people find learning kanji without context demotivating

**Best for:** People committed to the immersion approach who want to frontload kanji recognition before diving into native content.

#### WaniKani

**What it does:** Web-based SRS that teaches kanji with readings, meanings, and vocabulary. Uses radicals as building blocks and mnemonics for each character.

**Pros:**
- Teaches readings and vocabulary alongside kanji (not just meanings)
- Gamified, structured, low decision fatigue
- Active community
- Forced pacing prevents burnout from going too fast

**Cons:**
- Takes **12-18 months** at minimum to complete all 60 levels
- Paid subscription (~$9/month or $299 lifetime)
- Cannot speed up pace — stuck with its SRS intervals
- Locked into WaniKani's teaching order (not frequency-based)
- Some mnemonics are forced or unhelpful

**Best for:** Self-study learners who want a structured, all-in-one kanji + vocab system and don't mind the cost or timeline.

#### KKLC (Kodansha Kanji Learner's Course)

**What it does:** Teaches 2,300 kanji with meanings, readings, and curated vocabulary examples. Ordered by visual complexity and component relationships.

**Pros:**
- Teaches readings AND vocabulary (unlike RTK)
- Frequency-aware ordering
- Physical book — some learners prefer this
- One-time purchase, no subscription

**Cons:**
- More of a reference book than a guided method
- No built-in SRS — you need to pair it with Anki
- Slower than RTK if your goal is just kanji recognition
- Less community support than RTK or WaniKani

**Best for:** Learners who want a comprehensive kanji reference with context and are comfortable building their own Anki workflow.

#### The Opinionated Take

**For immersion learners:** Start with RTK (or its modern variant, Recognition RTK / Lazy Kanji) to build visual recognition in 2-3 months, then learn readings through vocabulary in context via sentence mining.

**For textbook learners:** WaniKani or KKLC alongside Genki. WaniKani if you want zero setup friction. KKLC if you want more control.

**For everyone:** Learning kanji purely in isolation (meaning only, no vocabulary) is incomplete. Learning kanji purely through vocabulary (no systematic study) is painfully slow. The best approach combines systematic kanji study with vocabulary learned in context.

### 1.4 Radical-Based Learning

All three major methods (RTK, WaniKani, KKLC) use radicals as building blocks. Radicals are the visual components that make up kanji.

Example: 休 (rest) = 人 (person) + 木 (tree) — a person leaning against a tree to rest.

Learning the ~214 traditional radicals (or the simplified set of ~60 most common ones) early on makes every subsequent kanji easier to learn, distinguish, and remember. WaniKani teaches its own set of radicals. RTK uses "primitives." KKLC uses "components." The concept is the same.

## 2. Grammar

### 2.1 Structure Overview

Japanese grammar is fundamentally different from English:

- **SOV word order:** "I sushi eat" not "I eat sushi" (私は寿司を食べる)
- **Particles:** Small words (は, が, を, に, で, etc.) that mark the grammatical role of each word. These are the backbone of Japanese grammar and the hardest part for English speakers.
- **Verb conjugation:** Verbs come at the end and conjugate for tense, negation, politeness, and conditionality. There are only two irregular verbs (する and 来る), but the conjugation system has many forms.
- **Topic-prominent language:** Japanese marks what you're talking about (topic, marked with は) separately from who does the action (subject, marked with が). English doesn't make this distinction.
- **Pro-drop:** Subjects and objects are routinely omitted when clear from context. "Ate sushi" is a complete Japanese sentence if the listener knows who ate it.

### 2.2 Keigo (Politeness Levels)

Japanese has multiple politeness registers:

- **Casual/plain form** (タメ口): Used with friends, family, in internal monologue. What manga and anime mostly use.
- **Polite/desu-masu form** (丁寧語): The default "safe" register. Used with strangers, acquaintances, in business. What textbooks teach first.
- **Honorific Japanese** (敬語): Includes 尊敬語 (sonkeigo, elevating others' actions) and 謙譲語 (kenjougo, humbling your own). Required in business and formal contexts.

**Practical advice:** Learn desu-masu form first (Genki teaches this). Understand plain form early (you need it for subordinate clauses and to understand media). Keigo can wait until intermediate level unless you're working in Japan.

### 2.3 Grammar Resources Compared

#### Genki I & II

**The textbook standard.** Used in most university Japanese courses. Covers roughly N5-N4 grammar.

- Structured, progressive, well-tested curriculum
- Good exercises, listening practice included
- Designed for classroom use — solo learners skip the pair activities
- Can feel slow if you're motivated
- ~$50 per volume, plus optional workbook

**Verdict:** The safe, proven choice. If you don't know what to pick, pick Genki.

#### Tae Kim's Guide to Learning Japanese (free)

- Available free online at guidetojapanese.org
- Explains grammar in plain English with good example sentences
- Covers N5 through N3 material
- No exercises or structured practice
- Some explanations are unconventional but effective

**Verdict:** Excellent free supplement. Many learners use Tae Kim alongside or instead of Genki.

#### Cure Dolly (YouTube, posthumous)

- Teaches Japanese grammar through a "Japanese from the ground up" approach using the concept of an invisible "A" car (が) as the core of every sentence
- Very different mental model from traditional textbooks
- Controversial audio quality and avatar presentation
- Some explanations are oversimplified or inaccurate according to critics
- Content creator passed away; content is static

**Verdict:** Watch the first 10-15 videos and see if the mental model clicks for you. Many learners find it transformative for understanding は vs が and sentence structure. Do not rely on it exclusively — cross-reference with other sources.

#### Bunpro

- Web-based SRS for grammar points (~$30/year)
- Covers N5 through N1
- Links to external grammar explanations (Tae Kim, Genki, etc.)
- Fill-in-the-blank SRS reviews
- Can follow textbook paths (Genki path, Tae Kim path, etc.)

**Verdict:** Best used as a review/reinforcement tool alongside a primary grammar resource, not as a standalone grammar teacher.

### 2.4 Grammar vs. Immersion Debate

The Japanese learning community is split:

**"Learn grammar systematically first" camp:**
- Grammar knowledge lets you parse sentences correctly from the start
- Prevents fossilized errors
- More efficient for comprehension
- Recommended: Work through Genki I & II or Tae Kim before heavy immersion

**"Acquire grammar through immersion" camp:**
- Grammar explanations are incomplete — real understanding comes from seeing patterns thousands of times
- Textbook grammar is artificially neat; real Japanese is messy
- Recommended: Learn minimal grammar, then learn through massive input

**The pragmatic consensus:** Do both. Get a foundation (N5-N4 grammar at minimum) from a structured resource, then let immersion refine and deepen your understanding. Trying to immerse with zero grammar is frustrating. Trying to learn all grammar from textbooks before immersing means you never immerse.

## 3. The Immersion Approach

### 3.1 AJATT / MIA / Refold — History and Current State

- **AJATT (All Japanese All The Time):** Created by "Khatzumoto" (~2006). The original immersion manifesto. Core idea: surround yourself with Japanese all day, every day. Bold, motivating, but vague on specifics.
- **MIA (Mass Immersion Approach):** Created by Matt vs Japan and Yoga (~2018). Formalized AJATT's ideas into a structured method with specific tools and stages. Dissolved in 2020 after a public falling-out between founders.
- **Refold:** Created by Matt vs Japan and Ethan (~2020). The current successor. Language-agnostic roadmap with four stages. More structured and commercial than AJATT. The dominant immersion framework as of 2026.

**The core ideas (shared by all three):**

1. **Comprehensible input is king.** You acquire language primarily by understanding messages, not by studying rules.
2. **Massive input hours.** You need thousands of hours of listening and reading to reach fluency.
3. **SRS for vocabulary.** Use Anki to retain words you encounter in immersion.
4. **Delay output.** Don't force speaking/writing too early — let comprehension build first.
5. **Tolerate ambiguity.** You won't understand everything. That's fine. Understanding improves gradually.

### 3.2 Sentence Mining

Sentence mining is the practice of creating Anki flashcards from sentences you encounter in native content (anime, manga, novels, news, etc.).

**The process:**
1. Consume native content (reading or listening)
2. When you encounter an unknown word in a sentence where you understand everything else (a "1T" or "i+1" sentence), look it up
3. Create an Anki card with the full sentence on the front and the word definition/reading on the back
4. Review daily via Anki's SRS algorithm

**Why sentence mining works:**
- Words are learned in real context, not isolation
- You build cards from content you care about (higher motivation, better retention)
- The surrounding sentence provides memory hooks
- You naturally learn grammar patterns alongside vocabulary

**When to start:** After learning ~1,000 basic words from a premade deck (like Core 2K) and finishing basic grammar (N5-N4). Mining with zero foundation is too frustrating — you'll have too many unknowns per sentence.

**Tools:** Yomitan (browser) + AnkiConnect for reading. Migaku or asbplayer for video content. See Section 7 for details.

### 3.3 Pitch Accent

Japanese is a **pitch-accent language.** Each word has a pattern of high and low pitch morae. Some minimal pairs exist: 橋 (はし, LH = bridge) vs 箸 (はし, HL = chopsticks).

**The community debate:**

**"Study it early" camp (Dogen, etc.):**
- Once you learn a word with wrong pitch, it's hard to fix
- Awareness of pitch patterns should be built from the start
- Even basic awareness dramatically improves natural-sounding speech

**"Don't worry about it" camp:**
- Most pitch errors don't cause misunderstanding
- You can acquire correct pitch through massive listening input
- Studying pitch accent rules is complex and overwhelming for beginners
- Many fluent foreigners in Japan have imperfect pitch and communicate fine

**Practical recommendation:**
1. **Learn what pitch accent IS** early on (spend 1-2 hours understanding the concept)
2. **Install pitch accent dictionaries in Yomitan** so you passively see pitch patterns when looking up words
3. **Do NOT make pitch accent a primary study focus** as a beginner — it's a distraction from higher-impact activities
4. **Revisit pitch accent seriously** at intermediate level (N3+) if natural-sounding pronunciation is a priority for you
5. **Dogen's pitch accent course** on Patreon is the gold standard if you decide to study it systematically

### 3.4 The "1,000 Hours of Input" Concept

The immersion community estimates that meaningful comprehension emerges around **1,000 hours of active listening input.** This doesn't mean 1,000 hours and you're fluent — it means this is roughly when you transition from "catching isolated words" to "following most of what's being said" in native content.

**Reality check:**
- 1,000 hours at 3 hours/day = ~11 months
- At 1.5 hours/day = ~22 months
- These are rough estimates — quality of attention, i+1 appropriateness, and supplementary study all affect the timeline
- Passive listening (background audio while doing other things) counts far less than active, focused listening
- Reading hours count separately and compound with listening — learners who read extensively often progress faster overall

## 4. JLPT Levels

The Japanese-Language Proficiency Test (JLPT) is administered twice yearly (July and December) worldwide. It's the most recognized Japanese proficiency certification.

### 4.1 Level Breakdown

| Level | Kanji | Vocabulary | Grammar Points | What You Can Do |
|-------|-------|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| **N5** | ~80 | ~800 | ~75 | Understand basic classroom phrases, self-introduction, very simple written sentences |
| **N4** | ~250 | ~1,500 | ~165 | Follow slow, simple conversations on daily topics. Read simple paragraphs. |
| **N3** | ~620 | ~3,750 | ~250 | Read and understand main points of newspaper headlines. Follow everyday conversations at natural speed. |
| **N2** | ~1,000 | ~6,000 | ~350 | Read newspapers and articles on general topics. Follow most native conversations. **Minimum for most Japanese jobs.** |
| **N1** | ~2,136 | ~10,000+ | ~450+ | Read complex texts (editorials, academic writing). Understand nuanced spoken Japanese. **Required for many professional roles.** |

### 4.2 Realistic Timelines

These are **cumulative hours from zero** for an English speaker with no prior CJK language knowledge:

| Level | Study Hours (Estimated) | Timeline at 2hr/day | Timeline at 3hr/day |
|-------|------------------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **N5** | 350-500 | 6-8 months | 4-6 months |
| **N4** | 550-800 | 9-13 months | 6-9 months |
| **N3** | 900-1,400 | 15-23 months | 10-16 months |
| **N2** | 1,500-2,200 | 2-3 years | 1.5-2 years |
| **N1** | 3,000-4,500 | 4-6 years | 3-4 years |

**Important caveats:**
- The jump from N3 to N2 is significantly harder than N4 to N3. Many learners stall here.
- The jump from N2 to N1 is even bigger — N1 covers more kanji than all other levels combined (1,136 additional kanji).
- These estimates assume focused, effective study. Inefficient methods (e.g., only using Duolingo) will take much longer.
- Immersion learners who supplement with structured study sometimes beat these timelines significantly.
- Living in Japan accelerates everything, especially listening and speaking.

### 4.3 Is the JLPT Worth Taking?

**Yes if:** You need it for a job in Japan, university admission, or visa purposes. Also useful as a concrete milestone to keep you motivated.

**No if:** You only care about actual ability. The JLPT has no speaking or writing section — it tests reading and listening only. Passing N2 doesn't mean you can hold a conversation. Conversely, many immersion learners who consume native content daily could pass N2 without specifically studying for it.

## 5. Common Beginner Mistakes

### 5.1 The Romaji Trap

**Mistake:** Using romaji (Latin alphabet) as a crutch instead of learning kana immediately.

**Why it hurts:** Romaji makes you process Japanese through English phonetics. Japanese sounds don't map 1:1 to English. Every day you spend reading romaji is a day you're not building kana fluency. Kana should be your first and most urgent task.

### 5.2 Direct Translation from English

**Mistake:** Constructing Japanese sentences by translating English word-for-word.

**Why it hurts:** Japanese sentence structure, word choice, and levels of explicitness are fundamentally different from English. "I eat sushi at the restaurant" doesn't translate word-by-word into natural Japanese. You need to think in Japanese patterns, not English patterns.

### 5.3 Overusing Pronouns

**Mistake:** Starting every sentence with 私は (watashi wa / "I").

**Why it hurts:** Japanese drops the subject when it's obvious from context. Constantly saying 私は sounds unnatural and childish to native speakers, like saying "I, me, I..." in English.

### 5.4 Particle Confusion (は vs が)

**Mistake:** Treating は (wa, topic marker) and が (ga, subject marker) as interchangeable.

**Why it hurts:** This is the single most nuanced aspect of Japanese grammar. は marks what you're talking about (topic). が marks who/what does the action or introduces new information. Getting them wrong changes meaning or sounds unnatural. Accept that this distinction will take years to fully internalize — but start learning it early.

### 5.5 Studying Kanji Readings in Isolation

**Mistake:** Memorizing that 生 can be read as せい, しょう, い(きる), う(まれる), なま, etc. without context.

**Why it hurts:** Many kanji have 5-10+ readings. Memorizing them all in isolation is futile. You learn readings by learning vocabulary words that use the kanji. When you learn 学生 (がくせい, student), you learn that 生 reads as せい in that context.

### 5.6 Perfectionism and Fear of Mistakes

**Mistake:** Refusing to use Japanese until you're "ready" or avoiding output because you might make errors.

**Why it hurts:** You'll never feel ready. Making mistakes is how you learn. The immersion community's advice to "delay output" means don't force production before you have comprehension — it doesn't mean wait until you're perfect.

### 5.7 Relying on a Single Resource

**Mistake:** Using only Duolingo, only an app, or only a textbook.

**Why it hurts:** No single resource covers everything. Duolingo alone will never get you to fluency. Apps lack depth. Textbooks lack real-world exposure. You need a combination: structured grammar study + SRS vocabulary + native content input + eventually, output practice.

### 5.8 Neglecting Listening

**Mistake:** Focusing exclusively on reading and writing, ignoring listening.

**Why it hurts:** Japanese spoken at natural speed sounds nothing like textbook audio. If you only read, you'll be unable to understand native speakers. Start listening to native content early, even if you understand almost nothing at first.

### 5.9 Giving Up at the Intermediate Plateau

**Mistake:** Quitting when progress seems to stall after the initial excitement fades (usually around N4-N3 level).

**Why it hurts:** Progress in language learning is not linear. The beginner phase feels fast because everything is new. The intermediate phase feels slow because the gap between "what you know" and "native content" is enormous. This is normal. Push through.

## 6. Recommended Learning Path

This is an opinionated, concrete path. It's based on what the most successful self-study learners actually do, combining structured study with immersion.

### Phase 1: Foundation (Months 1-2)

**Goal:** Learn all kana. Get basic grammar and vocabulary started. Set up your tools.

1. **Week 1-2:** Learn all hiragana and katakana using Tofugu's guide or similar mnemonic method. Practice writing by hand.
2. **Start Genki I** (or Tae Kim chapters 1-6 if you want free). Do one chapter per week.
3. **Install Japanese IME** on your computer and phone. Start typing in Japanese immediately.
4. **Set up Anki** and start the **Core 2K deck** (see Section 7). Do 10-20 new cards per day.
5. **Choose your kanji method:** Start WaniKani, or start RTK/RRTK, or start KKLC alongside Genki.
6. **Start passive listening:** Play Japanese podcasts or anime (without subs) in the background during commutes, chores, etc. You won't understand anything. That's fine — you're training your ear to the sounds of the language.

### Phase 2: Basic Grammar + Vocabulary Building (Months 3-6)

**Goal:** Finish N5-N4 grammar. Build vocabulary to ~1,500-2,000 words. Begin light immersion.

1. **Finish Genki I and start Genki II** (or equivalent Tae Kim / Bunpro material).
2. **Continue Anki:** Core 2K deck should be near completion. Maintain reviews.
3. **Continue kanji study:** If doing RTK, aim to finish or be well past halfway. If WaniKani, keep grinding levels.
4. **Start easy native content:**
   - **Graded readers** (Tadoku free graded readers, White Rabbit readers)
   - **NHK News Web Easy** (simplified news articles)
   - **Anime with Japanese subtitles** (NOT English subs — they become a crutch)
5. **Set up Yomitan** browser extension for reading practice.
6. **Optional:** Start Bunpro to SRS grammar points you've learned.

### Phase 3: Immersion Ramp-Up (Months 6-12)

**Goal:** Transition from textbook study to primarily learning from native content. Reach N3 level.

1. **Finish Genki II** or equivalent. You now have a grammar foundation sufficient for basic immersion.
2. **Start sentence mining:** Create Anki cards from native content you're consuming. Aim for 10-15 new cards per day.
3. **Increase immersion hours:**
   - 1+ hours of active listening per day (anime, podcasts, YouTube)
   - 30+ minutes of reading per day (manga, light novels, news, visual novels)
4. **Grammar as needed:** When you hit grammar patterns you don't understand, look them up on Bunpro, Tae Kim, or Japanese grammar dictionaries (Dictionary of Basic/Intermediate/Advanced Japanese Grammar — the "green/blue/red" books).
5. **Continue kanji study** until you've covered ~1,000-1,500 characters.

### Phase 4: Intermediate Grind (Months 12-24)

**Goal:** Build deep comprehension through massive input. Reach N2 level.

1. **Immersion is now your primary study method.** 2-3+ hours daily of reading and listening.
2. **Continue sentence mining** from increasingly difficult content.
3. **Read your first novel or light novel** in Japanese (pick something you're genuinely interested in — motivation matters more than difficulty level).
4. **Start output practice:** Find a language exchange partner (HelloTalk, Tandem, iTalki) or join a Japanese Discord server. Begin speaking and writing.
5. **Study N2 grammar** systematically using Bunpro or the "blue" Dictionary of Intermediate Japanese Grammar.
6. **Optional:** Study pitch accent basics if pronunciation quality matters to you (Dogen's course on Patreon).

### Phase 5: Advanced (Months 24+)

**Goal:** Approach native-level comprehension. Pass N1 if desired.

1. **Read widely:** Novels, news, essays, manga across genres. Breadth matters.
2. **Listen to diverse content:** Talk shows, podcasts, audiobooks, lectures — not just anime.
3. **Refine output:** Regular conversation practice, writing practice (Lang-8, HelloTalk).
4. **Study N1 grammar** and low-frequency vocabulary.
5. **Specialize:** Focus on the domains you care about (business Japanese, academic Japanese, casual conversation, etc.).

## 7. Software and Tools

### 7.1 Japanese IME (Input Method Editor)

You need this to type Japanese on your computer. It converts romaji keystrokes into hiragana/katakana/kanji.

**Windows:**
- Settings > Time & Language > Language & Region > Add Japanese
- Switch with `Alt + ~` (backtick) or `Win + Space`
- Type romaji, press Space to convert to kanji

**Mac:**
- System Settings > Keyboard > Input Sources > Add Japanese (Romaji)
- Switch with `Ctrl + Space` or `Fn`
- Same romaji-to-kana-to-kanji conversion

**Linux:**
- Install `fcitx5` with `fcitx5-mozc` (best option for most distros)
- `ibus-mozc` also works

**Phone:** Add Japanese keyboard in your phone's keyboard settings. Both iOS and Android have built-in Japanese input (flick keyboard is standard in Japan, but romaji keyboard works too).

**Set this up on Day 1.** Typing Japanese regularly is essential practice.

### 7.2 Anki + Japanese Decks

**Anki** (free on desktop/Android, $25 on iOS) is a spaced repetition flashcard app. It is the single most important tool for Japanese learning after a grammar resource.

**Essential decks:**

- **Core 2K/6K:** Vocabulary decks based on word frequency. Core 2K covers the 2,000 most common words with native audio and example sentences. Start here. The 6K and 10K extensions continue with less common vocabulary.
- **Kanji decks:** If using RTK, get the "All in One Kanji Heisig RTK" deck. If using KKLC, find the KKLC Anki deck. WaniKani users don't need a kanji deck.
- **Sentence mining deck:** You'll build this yourself from native content (see Section 3.2).
- **Tango N5/N4/N3:** Alternative to Core 2K — organized by JLPT level with example sentences. Popular in the Refold community.

**Anki settings recommendations (for Japanese):**
- New cards per day: 10-20 (don't go higher — reviews compound fast)
- Learning steps: 1m 10m
- Graduating interval: 1 day
- Maximum reviews/day: no limit (unlimited — skipping reviews defeats the purpose)

### 7.3 Yomitan Browser Extension

**Yomitan** (successor to Yomichan) is a browser extension for Chrome/Firefox that provides instant dictionary pop-ups when you hover over Japanese text. It is transformative for reading practice.

**Key features:**
- Hover over any Japanese word to see definitions, readings, pitch accent
- Supports multiple dictionaries simultaneously (JMdict, Kenkyusha, etc.)
- One-click Anki card creation via AnkiConnect
- Customizable card templates

**Setup:**
1. Install Yomitan from your browser's extension store
2. Install AnkiConnect plugin in Anki
3. Download dictionary files from the Yomitan wiki (at minimum: JMdict, KANJIDIC)
4. Recommended additions: pitch accent dictionary (for pitch accent display), frequency lists (to know how common a word is)

**This is the single most impactful tool for the reading-based immersion approach.** Set it up before you start reading native content.

### 7.4 jpdb.io

**jpdb.io** is a free web-based vocabulary learning system with a unique strength: it has pre-analyzed the vocabulary of thousands of anime, manga, light novels, visual novels, and dramas.

**Why it's useful:**
- Look up any anime/manga/novel and see exactly what vocabulary it uses, sorted by frequency
- Pre-learn vocabulary for specific content before you watch/read it
- Built-in SRS for vocabulary review
- Can import your known words from Anki
- Difficulty ratings for content help you choose appropriate immersion material

**Best used for:** Planning your immersion content pipeline and pre-studying vocabulary for specific shows/books.

### 7.5 Jalup (Japanese Level Up)

A paid app (~$10/pack) that teaches Japanese through an i+1 sentence progression — each sentence introduces exactly one new element. Covers beginner through advanced.

**Pros:** Very structured, good for people who want a guided sentence-based approach.
**Cons:** Expensive for the full set, less flexible than building your own Anki deck.

### 7.6 Migaku

A browser extension and toolkit focused on language learning from video content. Supports subtitle-based sentence mining, vocabulary highlighting, and Anki card creation.

**Best for:** Learners who immerse primarily through video (anime, drama, YouTube).

### 7.7 Other Useful Tools

- **Jisho.org** — Clean, fast online Japanese-English dictionary. Good for quick lookups.
- **Takoboto** (Android) / **Shirabe Jisho** (iOS) — Offline Japanese dictionaries for mobile.
- **Bunpro** — SRS for grammar (see Section 2.3).
- **HelloTalk / Tandem** — Language exchange apps for finding Japanese conversation partners.
- **iTalki** — Hire professional Japanese tutors for conversation practice ($10-30/hour).
- **Satori Reader** — Graded reading content with built-in dictionary and grammar notes. Good bridge between textbook and native content.
- **Tadoku graded readers** — Free online graded readers for absolute beginners.
- **NHK News Web Easy** — Simplified Japanese news articles with furigana (reading aids).
- **Immersion Kit** (immersionkit.com) — Search for Japanese sentences from anime/drama with audio, video clips, and context. Great for finding example sentences.

## 8. Summary: What Actually Works

Based on thousands of community success stories across r/LearnJapanese, Refold, WaniKani forums, and immersion communities, here is what consistently produces results:

1. **Learn kana immediately.** No romaji. Ever.
2. **Use Anki daily.** Consistency beats intensity. 20 minutes of daily reviews beats 3-hour weekend cramming sessions.
3. **Get a grammar foundation** (Genki or Tae Kim through N4) before trying to immerse heavily.
4. **Start a kanji system early** and stick with it. RTK, WaniKani, or KKLC — picking one and finishing it matters more than picking the "best" one.
5. **Immerse as early as possible,** even when you understand almost nothing. Your tolerance for ambiguity is a skill you need to develop.
6. **Read, read, read.** Reading (with Yomitan) is the highest-density input activity. It builds vocabulary, reinforces kanji, and exposes you to grammar patterns faster than listening alone.
7. **Sentence mine from content you enjoy.** Motivation is the #1 predictor of success. If you hate anime, don't force yourself to watch anime. Read manga, play games, watch dramas — whatever keeps you coming back.
8. **Don't optimize prematurely.** The best method is the one you actually do consistently. Spending weeks researching the "optimal" approach is weeks not spent learning Japanese.
9. **Expect it to take years.** Japanese is genuinely one of the hardest languages for English speakers. Anyone who tells you otherwise is selling something. But it is absolutely learnable — thousands of English speakers reach fluency through self-study every year.
