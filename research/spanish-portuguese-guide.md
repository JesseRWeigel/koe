## 1. Spanish for Travel & Conversation

### How Quickly Can You Reach Conversational Level?

The FSI (Foreign Service Institute) classifies Spanish as a **Category I language** -- the easiest tier for English speakers -- requiring **600-750 classroom hours** for professional working proficiency (ILR Level 3 / CEFR C1).

However, **conversational fluency is a much lower bar** than professional proficiency:

| Goal | Approximate Hours | Timeline (2 hrs/day) |
|------|------------------|----------------------|
| Survival tourist Spanish | 50-80 hrs | 1-2 months |
| Basic conversational (A2) | 150-200 hrs | 3-4 months |
| Solid conversational (B1) | 300-400 hrs | 5-7 months |
| Upper intermediate (B2) | 500-600 hrs | 8-12 months |
| Professional proficiency (C1) | 600-750 hrs | 12-15 months |

**Realistic expectation:** With focused daily practice (1-2 hours), you can hold a 15-minute conversation with a native speaker in **3 months** and feel comfortable in most travel situations in **4-6 months**.

### Most Important Vocabulary & Phrases for Travel

Learning the **most common 1,000-2,000 words** gives you comprehension of roughly 80% of everyday conversation. Prioritize these categories:

**Survival Phrases (learn first week):**
- Greetings: Hola, Buenos dias, Buenas tardes, Buenas noches
- Politeness: Por favor, Gracias, De nada, Disculpe, Perdon
- Basics: Si, No, Tal vez, No entiendo, Puede repetir?
- Questions: Donde esta...?, Cuanto cuesta?, Tiene...?, Que es esto?
- Emergency: Necesito ayuda, Estoy perdido/a, Llamar la policia/ambulancia

**High-Value Travel Vocabulary (first month):**
- Numbers 1-1000, days of the week, months
- Directions: izquierda, derecha, recto, cerca, lejos
- Food ordering: La cuenta, Quiero..., Sin [allergen], Menu, Propina
- Transportation: boleto, parada, aeropuerto, taxi, estacion
- Accommodation: habitacion, reserva, llave, bano, toalla
- Shopping: efectivo, tarjeta, descuento, talla, cambio

**Conversational Connectors (game-changers for sounding natural):**
- Bueno, Entonces, Pues, O sea, Es que...
- Me parece que..., La verdad es que..., No se si...
- Que bueno!, Que lastima!, En serio?

### Best Accelerated Learning Approaches

- Features 15+ native speakers from diverse Spanish-speaking regions

- **Best for:** Listening comprehension, natural acquisition, accent development
- **Limitation:** No structured speaking practice; the pure CI approach is slow if used alone
- **Verdict:** Excellent as a *supplement* but insufficient as your *only* method if speed matters

- Uses the "Thinking Method" -- no memorization, builds understanding of *how* Spanish works
- Completely free with no upselling

- **Limitation:** No visual/written materials; won't make you fluent alone
- **Verdict:** Best free resource for getting started. Complete it in 2-3 weeks alongside other methods

**3. 1-on-1 Tutoring (italki, Preply, Baselang)**
- **The single fastest path to speaking ability** -- a good tutor gets you speaking 70%+ of the lesson
- Baselang offers unlimited tutoring for a flat monthly fee (~$149/mo)
- italki: book individual sessions with tutors from any country ($5-25/hr)

- **Verdict:** Non-negotiable if you want to speak. Even 2-3 sessions per week accelerates progress dramatically

- Combine with sentences, not isolated words

**6. Conversation Exchange Partners**
- Tandem, HelloTalk, or local meetups
- Free speaking practice with native speakers who want to learn English

#### Tier 3: Immersion Boosters

### Latin American Spanish Dialects

If you're learning for travel in Latin America, understanding dialectal differences helps set expectations.

**Mexican Spanish**
- The most widely understood dialect due to media influence
- Clear pronunciation, moderate speed
- Uses "tu" for informal "you"
- Vocabulary influenced by Nahuatl (aguacate, chocolate, tomate)
- Good default choice for learners targeting Latin America

**Colombian Spanish (Bogota)**
- Often cited as one of the "clearest" dialects
- Distinct from coastal Colombian Spanish (which is faster, drops more consonants)
- Good for learners who want very clear pronunciation models

**Argentine/Rioplatense Spanish**
- Heavy Italian influence on intonation (sounds almost Italian at times)
- Uses "vos" instead of "tu" (vos tenes instead of tu tienes)
- "LL" and "Y" pronounced as "sh" (calle = "CA-she")
- Quite distinctive -- may cause confusion if you learn this first and travel elsewhere

**Central American Spanish (El Salvador, Guatemala, Honduras)**
- Syllable-final "s" frequently dropped or aspirated (estas = "ehtah")
- Some regions use "vos" (notably El Salvador, Guatemala)
- Generally faster, more casual register
- Less media representation means fewer learning resources

**Caribbean Spanish (Cuba, Dominican Republic, Puerto Rico)**
- Very fast, drops many consonants
- "S" at end of syllables often disappears entirely
- "R" and "L" interchange in some positions
- Considered harder to understand for beginners

**Recommendation:** Start with Mexican or neutral Latin American Spanish. It's the most widely understood and has the most learning resources. You'll adapt to regional accents naturally through exposure.

### Common Mistakes English Speakers Make

**Pronunciation Pitfalls:**

1. **Mushy vowels.** Spanish has exactly 5 pure vowel sounds (a, e, i, o, u) -- always crisp, never reduced to schwa. English speakers tend to muddy unstressed vowels
2. **Not rolling the R.** Spanish has two R sounds: a single tap (pero) and a trill (perro). The trill is mandatory in certain positions -- practice early
3. **Aspirating P, T, K.** In English, these consonants get a puff of air (hold paper in front of mouth saying "pin"). In Spanish, no aspiration -- softer, cleaner
4. **Pronouncing the H.** The H in Spanish is **always silent**. "Hola" = "ola"
5. **English stress patterns on cognates.** "Animal" = ah-nee-MAL (not AN-i-mul). "Hotel" = oh-TEL. "Chocolate" = cho-co-LA-te

**Grammar Pitfalls:**

1. **Ignoring gender agreement.** Every noun is masculine or feminine -- adjectives and articles must match. La casa blancA, el carro blancO
2. **Overusing subject pronouns.** "Yo quiero" is redundant -- "Quiero" is natural. Spanish verbs already encode the subject
3. **Ser vs. Estar confusion.** Both mean "to be" but are not interchangeable. Ser = identity/essence. Estar = state/location. "Estoy aburrido" (I'm bored) vs "Soy aburrido" (I'm a boring person)
4. **False cognates.** "Embarazada" = pregnant (not embarrassed). "Actual" = current (not actual). "Sensible" = sensitive (not sensible)
5. **Direct translation of English structures.** "I am 30 years old" = "Tengo 30 anos" (I *have* 30 years), not "Soy 30 anos"

## 2. Brazilian Portuguese

### Brazilian vs. European Portuguese

These are the **same language** but with significant differences -- comparable to British vs. American English, but more pronounced. They are mutually intelligible, though Brazilians sometimes struggle with European Portuguese more than the reverse.

**Pronunciation Differences:**

| Feature | Brazilian Portuguese | European Portuguese |
|---------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Overall sound | More melodic, open, "musical" | More guttural, clipped, "Slavic-sounding" |
| Unstressed vowels | Fully pronounced, open | Reduced or swallowed entirely |
| Letter R (initial/double) | Pronounced like English "H" | Rolled/trilled like Spanish |
| Final S | Pronounced as "S" | Pronounced as "SH" |
| Letter T (before I) | Pronounced "CH" (tia = "CHEE-ah") | Pronounced as "T" (tia = "TEE-ah") |
| Letter D (before I) | Pronounced "J" (dia = "JEE-ah") | Pronounced as "D" |

**Vocabulary Differences:**

| English | Brazilian Portuguese | European Portuguese |
|---------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Train | trem | comboio |
| Bus | onibus | autocarro |
| Breakfast | cafe da manha | pequeno-almoco |
| Cell phone | celular | telemovel |
| Sweater | sueter | pulover |
| Pedestrian | pedestre | peao |
| Ice cream | sorvete | gelado |

**Grammar Differences:**
- **"You"**: Brazil uses "voce" universally for informal; Portugal uses "tu" (with distinct verb conjugations)
- **Gerund usage**: Brazil says "estou fazendo" (I am doing); Portugal says "estou a fazer"
- **Pronoun placement**: Brazil puts pronouns before verbs ("me da"); Portugal after ("da-me")

**Bottom line:** If your goal is Brazil, learn **Brazilian Portuguese specifically**. Resources, pronunciation, vocabulary, and even grammar conventions differ enough that learning European Portuguese will cause confusion and sound unnatural in Brazil.

### Workplace/Professional Portuguese

Brazilian business culture is **relationship-first** -- understanding cultural norms is as important as the language itself.

**Key Professional Phrases:**

| Situation | Portuguese | Literal Meaning |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Meeting someone | Muito prazer | Much pleasure (my pleasure) |
| How are you? (formal) | Como vai? | How goes it? |
| How are you? (casual) | Tudo bem? | Everything good? |
| I agree | Concordo / Estou de acordo | I agree / I'm in accord |
| I'd like to suggest | Gostaria de sugerir | I would like to suggest |
| Could you explain? | Poderia explicar? | Could you explain? |
| Let's schedule a meeting | Vamos marcar uma reuniao | Let's set a meeting |
| Pleased to meet you | Prazer em conhece-lo/la | Pleasure in meeting you |
| Thank you for your time | Obrigado/a pelo seu tempo | Thankful for your time |

**Cultural Norms for the Workplace:**

1. **Titles matter.** Use Senhor/Senhora + first name (not surname). Professional titles like "Doutor" or "Professor" are expected
2. **Physical greetings.** Handshakes for men; women receive kisses on both cheeks even in business settings. This is normal, not overly familiar
3. **Punctuality is flexible.** Arrive on time yourself, but don't be surprised if Brazilian colleagues are late. Meetings may start 15-30 minutes behind
4. **Relationship before business.** Expect small talk and personal questions before getting to the agenda. Jumping straight to business feels cold and rude
5. **Meetings are non-linear.** Agendas are loose; people interrupt with ideas freely. This isn't disrespect -- it's engagement
6. **"Jeitinho brasileiro"** -- "the Brazilian way around" -- describes a cultural tendency to find creative workarounds to rigid rules. Understanding this concept helps navigate bureaucracy and business processes
7. **Business cards.** Have them printed with Portuguese on one side. Present Portuguese side up
8. **Indirect communication.** Brazilians avoid direct confrontation. A soft "vamos ver" (let's see) or "e dificil" (it's difficult) often means "no"

### Brazilian Culture Context for Language Learning

Understanding culture accelerates language acquisition because it gives you **context for why things are said the way they are.**

- **Warmth and physical closeness.** Brazilians stand closer, touch more, and use diminutives (inho/inha) to express affection. "Obrigadinho" instead of "obrigado" is endearing, not childish
- **Music is a superpower for learning.** Brazilian music (MPB, sertanejo, bossa nova, funk, samba) is globally loved. Songs by Gilberto Gil, Caetano Veloso, Marisa Monte, and Jorge Ben Jor are excellent listening practice
- **Telenovelas.** Brazilian soap operas are a cultural institution and fantastic for immersion. They use natural, everyday speech
- **Football vocabulary.** Knowing basic football (soccer) terms opens instant rapport with most Brazilians
- **Regional diversity.** Brazil is enormous. Accents vary from the Carioca (Rio) to Paulista (Sao Paulo) to Nordestino (Northeast). Sao Paulo accent is considered the "broadcast standard"

### Can Spanish Knowledge Help Learn Portuguese?

**Absolutely yes -- it's one of the biggest advantages you can have.**

Spanish and Portuguese share **89% lexical similarity** -- 9 out of 10 words have a recognizable cognate. A Spanish speaker reading Portuguese text can often understand 50-70% immediately.

**Where Spanish helps enormously:**
- Vocabulary: The vast majority of words are recognizable (casa, familia, importante, necesitar/necessitar)
- Grammar structure: Subject-verb-object, gendered nouns, verb conjugation patterns are very similar
- Reading comprehension: A Spanish speaker can read Portuguese newspapers with surprising comprehension
- Cultural familiarity: Latin/Romance cultural frameworks are shared

**Where Spanish can mislead you:**

The **false cognates** ("false friends") between Spanish and Portuguese are the biggest trap:

| Spanish | Meaning | Portuguese | Meaning |
|---------|---------|-----------|---------|
| embarazada | pregnant | embaracada | embarrassed |
| largo | long | largo | wide |
| borracha | drunk (f.) | borracha | rubber/eraser |
| exquisito | exquisite/delicious | esquisito | strange/weird |
| polvo | dust | polvo | octopus |
| pegar | to stick/glue | pegar | to grab/catch |
| propina | tip (money) | propina | bribe |
| salsa | sauce | salsa | parsley |
| vaso | glass (drinking) | vaso | vase |
| apellido | surname | apelido | nickname |

**Key structural differences a Spanish speaker must unlearn:**
- Portuguese has far more vowel sounds (7 oral + 5 nasal vs. Spanish's 5 oral)
- Portuguese contractions are mandatory (de + o = do, em + a = na) whereas Spanish keeps them separate
- Portuguese uses personal infinitives (a unique feature among Romance languages)
- Ser/Estar distinction exists in both, but some usages differ

**Verdict:** Learn Spanish first to at least A2/B1, then Portuguese becomes dramatically easier. The FSI estimates Portuguese at 575-600 hours for English speakers, but Spanish speakers commonly report reaching conversational Portuguese in **100-200 hours**.

### Pronunciation Challenges

Portuguese has one of the **richest vowel systems** of any Romance language. This is the single hardest aspect for English (and Spanish) speakers.

**Nasal Vowels**

Nasal vowels are produced by allowing air through both mouth and nose simultaneously. They don't exist in English or Spanish. In Portuguese, vowels are nasalized when:
- Followed by M or N in the same syllable
- Marked with a tilde (~)

The five nasal vowels:
- **a** (nasal) -- as in "manha" (morning) -- like "uh" but through the nose
- **e** (nasal) -- as in "tempo" (time) -- like "eh" nasalized
- **i** (nasal) -- as in "sim" (yes) -- like "ee" nasalized
- **o** (nasal) -- as in "bom" (good) -- like "oh" nasalized
- **u** (nasal) -- as in "um" (one) -- like "oo" nasalized

**The "-ao" Diphthong**

Arguably the most challenging sound in Portuguese. It combines a nasal "a" gliding into a nasal "u" sound. Found in extremely common words:
- nao (no), pao (bread), mao (hand), coracao (heart), informacao (information)
- Every third-person plural past tense: falaram, comeram, viveram

**Practice tip:** Say "ow" (as in "now") but route the sound through your nose, closing off the back of your mouth slightly. It takes weeks of practice to feel natural.

**The "LH" Sound**

Pronounced similar to the "lli" in English "million" or the Spanish "ll" in some dialects. Examples:
- trabalho (work) = "tra-BA-lyo"
- filho (son) = "FEE-lyo"
- olho (eye) = "OH-lyo"

**The "NH" Sound**

Equivalent to Spanish "n" or the "ny" in English "canyon." Examples:
- minha (my, f.) = "MEE-nya"
- banho (bath) = "BA-nyo"
- senhora (ma'am) = "se-NYO-ra"

**Other Tricky Sounds:**
- **R at start of words or doubled RR** = "H" sound. "Rio" = "HEE-oo", "carro" = "CA-hoo"
- **Final R** = varies by region. Sao Paulo: American-style R. Rio: "H" sound. Northeast: tapped R
- **T before I** = "CH" sound. "Tia" (aunt) = "CHEE-ah"
- **D before I** = "J" sound. "Dia" (day) = "JEE-ah"
- **Final E** = often pronounced as "ee". "Cidade" = "see-DA-jee"

## 3. Learning Multiple Languages Simultaneously

### Is It Advisable?

**The research is mixed, but leans toward "it depends on your level."**

**Arguments for simultaneous learning:**
- Polyglots like Luca Lampariello, Steve Kaufmann, and Benny Lewis have all reported success
- Your brain's language-learning "muscle" strengthens with each additional language
- Similar languages share structures, so learning one genuinely helps the other
- Keeps things interesting and prevents burnout from a single language

**Arguments against (especially for beginners):**
- **Interference is real** -- Spanish and Portuguese are so similar (89% lexical overlap) that mixing them is almost guaranteed at early stages
- Splitting study time means slower progress in each language
- Beginners haven't yet built the "language container" that keeps languages separate
- Research suggests languages compete for the same memory pathways when similarity is high

### How to Avoid Interference Between Spanish and Portuguese

This is **the** central challenge of learning these two simultaneously.

**1. Separate by Time of Day**
Study Spanish in the morning, Portuguese in the evening (or vice versa). Never in the same sitting. Your brain needs distinct "context windows" for each.

**2. Use Different Modalities**
- Spanish: audio-first (Language Transfer, podcasts)
- Portuguese: visual-first (reading, flashcards, videos)
- This creates distinct memory pathways

**3. Use Parallel Materials**
Study the same topic in both languages. When you learn "food vocabulary" in Spanish, do the same in Portuguese the same week. This forces your brain to notice the *differences*, which is what prevents mixing.

**4. Focus Hard on Pronunciation**
Since the grammars are so similar, **pronunciation is your main differentiator.** Exaggerate the differences:
- Portuguese nasal vowels vs. Spanish pure vowels
- Portuguese "R" = "H" vs. Spanish rolled R
- Portuguese rhythm (stress-timed) vs. Spanish rhythm (syllable-timed)

**5. Study False Cognates Deliberately**
Make flashcards specifically for false friends. These are the words that will trip you up most.

**6. Use Visual/Environmental Anchors**
- Study Spanish at your desk, Portuguese on the couch
- Use different colored notebooks
- Different Anki decks with different card styles
- Different music playlists for each language's study sessions

### Strategies for Managing Multiple Languages

**Laddering**
Use your stronger language to learn the weaker one. Once you have A2 Spanish, start learning Portuguese *through Spanish* rather than through English. This reinforces Spanish while building Portuguese, and leverages the 89% similarity.

**Time-Boxing**
- **Week rotation**: 4 days Spanish / 3 days Portuguese (or whatever ratio matches your priority)
- **Block scheduling**: Monday/Wednesday/Friday = Spanish, Tuesday/Thursday/Saturday = Portuguese
- **Never less than 3 days per week** for either language, or you'll lose momentum

**The "One Active, One Maintenance" Model**
- Actively study Language A (new material, tutoring, grammar)
- Maintain Language B (review, input, casual listening)
- Switch every 2-4 weeks

### When to Start the Second Language

**The consensus recommendation:**

1. **Reach A2-B1 in your first language before starting the second.** This means you can:
   - Have basic conversations
   - Understand simple texts
   - Use present, past, and future tenses
   - Handle common situations (ordering food, asking directions, basic small talk)

2. For Spanish/Portuguese specifically, most experts recommend **getting Spanish to B1 first, then adding Portuguese.** Reasons:
   - Spanish is slightly easier phonetically (fewer vowel sounds)
   - Spanish has more learning resources
   - Spanish pronunciation habits are easier to maintain while adding Portuguese
   - Portuguese speakers typically understand Spanish better than vice versa
   - A B1 Spanish speaker can reach conversational Portuguese much faster (100-200 hours vs. 575+ from zero)

3. **Minimum timeline:** Study Spanish exclusively for **4-6 months** before introducing Portuguese

## 4. Conversational Fluency Shortcuts

### What Gets You to "Functional Conversation" Fastest

"Functional conversation" means: you can have a 15-20 minute unscripted conversation on everyday topics, understand most of what's said to you (asking for repetition sometimes), and express your thoughts even if imperfectly.

### For Spanish: The 90-Day Sprint

**Week 1-2: Foundation**
- Complete Language Transfer (free, ~15 hours total) for grammar intuition
- Learn top 200 words via Anki frequency deck
- Master pronunciation fundamentals (vowels, R trill, no aspiration)
- Start Dreaming Spanish at Superbeginner level (30 min/day)

**Week 3-6: Build the Core**
- Continue Anki (now top 500-1000 words)
- Start 2-3 tutor sessions/week on italki (focus on survival conversations)
- Increase Dreaming Spanish to Beginner level (45 min/day)
- Begin SpanishPod101 beginner pathway
- Change phone to Spanish

**Week 7-10: Activate Speaking**
- Increase tutor sessions to 3-4/week
- Add conversation exchange partner (Tandem/HelloTalk)
- Start watching Netflix shows in Spanish with Spanish subtitles
- Practice ordering food, asking directions, and small talk in tutoring
- Write a daily journal (5-10 sentences)

**Week 11-13: Push to Conversational**
- Reduce Anki, increase speaking time
- Do "monologue practice" -- talk to yourself in Spanish for 5 min daily about your day
- Attempt 15-minute unscripted conversations with tutor
- Listen to podcasts at natural speed
- Celebrate: you should be able to sustain basic conversations

**Key metrics at 90 days (~150 hours):**
- 1,000-1,500 active vocabulary words
- Present, past (preterite + imperfect), and future tenses
- Can handle restaurants, transportation, shopping, basic social situations
- Understand 50-60% of natural speech at moderate speed

### For Brazilian Portuguese: The 90-Day Sprint

*(Adjust if you already know Spanish -- you can compress this to 60 days)*

**Week 1-2: Sound System First**
- Portuguese pronunciation is harder than Spanish -- start here
- Drill nasal vowels, the "ao" diphthong, R-as-H, T-as-CH, D-as-J
- Use Speechling or Speaking Brazilian YouTube for pronunciation
- Learn top 200 words, focusing on how they *sound* different from Spanish cognates
- Start Pimsleur Brazilian Portuguese (audio-based, pronunciation-focused)

**Week 3-6: Build the Core**
- Continue Pimsleur or Rocket Portuguese
- Start tutor sessions on italki (2-3/week)
- Begin PortuguesePod101 or BrazilianPodClass
- Anki with top 500-1000 words
- Watch Brazilian telenovelas or YouTube with Portuguese subtitles

**Week 7-10: Activate Speaking**
- Increase tutoring to 3-4 sessions/week
- Practice professional phrases if workplace is a priority
- Add Tandem/HelloTalk for casual conversation practice
- Listen to Brazilian music -- look up lyrics, sing along (great for nasal vowels)
- Start reading simple news (Folha de S.Paulo has simpler articles)

**Week 11-13: Push to Conversational**
- Extended conversation practice with tutor
- Monologue practice (describe your day, opinions on topics)
- Try to watch content without subtitles
- Practice workplace scenarios if relevant

### Universal Shortcuts That Work for Both Languages

1. **The "100 sentence" method.** Learn 100 carefully chosen sentences (not words) that cover 80% of conversation situations. Sentences encode grammar, vocabulary, and natural phrasing simultaneously

2. **Shadowing.** Listen to native speech and repeat immediately, mimicking tone, rhythm, and pronunciation. Do 10-15 minutes daily. This builds muscle memory and listening skills simultaneously

3. **The "glossika" effect.** Mass repetition of sentences at spaced intervals. Even if you use Anki with full sentences instead of the Glossika product, the principle works: repeat thousands of sentences and patterns internalize automatically

4. **Talk to yourself.** Narrate your actions in the target language throughout the day. "I'm making coffee. The coffee is hot. I need milk." This activates vocabulary in context without needing a partner

5. **Focus on verbs, not nouns.** Nouns can be pointed at or looked up. Verbs are the skeleton of communication. Master the 50 most common verbs in present, past, and future and you can express almost anything

6. **Use circumlocution.** Don't know the word for "refrigerator"? Say "the cold box for food" (la caja fria para comida). Native speakers do this too. This skill lets you communicate *immediately* with limited vocabulary

7. **Learn filler words.** "Bueno..." "Pues..." "Entonces..." (Spanish) / "Bom..." "Entao..." "Tipo..." (Portuguese). These buy you thinking time and make you sound more natural

8. **Prioritize high-frequency irregular verbs.** In both languages, the most common verbs are the most irregular: ser, estar, ter/tener, ir, fazer/hacer, poder, saber, querer. Learn these first

| Activity | Impact on Speaking | Time Investment | Priority |

## Recommended Learning Path (Both Languages)

### Months 1-6: Spanish Focus
- Spanish as primary language (80% of study time)
- Goal: Reach B1 (solid conversational ability)
- Method: Language Transfer + tutoring + Dreaming Spanish + Anki

### Months 4-6: Portuguese Introduction
- Begin Portuguese exposure (20% of study time)
- Pronunciation-only phase -- drill sounds that differ from Spanish
- Passive listening to Brazilian music and podcasts
- No active grammar study yet

### Months 7-12: Dual Track
- Spanish maintenance (40% of study time) -- tutoring, immersion content
- Portuguese active learning (60% of study time) -- leverage Spanish knowledge
- Goal: Spanish B1-B2, Portuguese A2-B1

### Month 12+: Balance
- Both languages in maintenance + growth mode
- Travel to Spanish-speaking and Portuguese-speaking environments
- Professional Portuguese development if needed
